7 things to know before buying your first home

Buying your first home is one of life’s most exciting milestones. But with that excitement comes a mix of paperwork, emotions, and big financial decisions. Whether you're just starting your search or already visiting open houses, knowing what to expect makes all the difference.

Here are 7 key things every first-time homebuyer should know to feel confident and prepared.

1. Know your full budget before falling in love with a home

It’s easy to get emotionally attached to listings, but affordability must come first. Your true budget includes more than the home's sticker price. You’ll need to factor in:

  • Down payment (typically 10–20%)
  • Monthly mortgage payments
  • Property taxes and homeowners insurance
  • Closing costs (usually 2–5% of the price)
  • Ongoing maintenance and emergency repairs

Getting clear on these numbers early helps you avoid financial strain down the line.

2. Get pre-approved, not just pre-qualified

While pre-qualification gives you a rough estimate of your borrowing power, pre-approval is much stronger. It means a lender has reviewed your credit, income, and financials and agreed to lend you a specific amount—subject to final verification.

This makes your offers more attractive to sellers and shows you're ready to move fast.

3. Don’t just shop for a house—shop the neighborhood

You’re not just buying four walls; you’re buying into a lifestyle. That’s why the surrounding area matters just as much as the property itself. Spend time exploring neighborhoods, both online and in person. Drive around at different times of day, talk to locals, and check commute routes.

Look into:

  • School ratings
  • Walkability and amenities
  • Future developments or construction
  • Crime rates and safety reports

A great home in the wrong location rarely leads to long-term happiness.

4. Work with a reliable real estate agent

Having a trusted agent by your side can save you time, money, and stress. For first-time buyers, this is especially important—they’ll guide you through each stage and advocate for your best interests.

A good agent will:

  • Recommend listings that match your needs
  • Explain market trends in simple terms
  • Negotiate on your behalf
  • Help with inspections, paperwork, and closing

Think of them as your personal real estate coach.

5. Don’t skip the home inspection

Even if the home looks perfect, never waive the inspection. A professional inspection reveals issues you can’t see—saving you from expensive surprises later.

Common things inspections uncover:

  • Roof damage or leaks
  • Plumbing or electrical issues
  • Structural cracks
  • Mold, pests, or drainage problems

Use the results to negotiate repairs or price adjustments before finalizing the deal.

6. Expect additional costs after you move in

Many first-time buyers are surprised by the expenses that show up after the purchase. Budgeting only for the down payment and monthly mortgage can leave you financially stretched.

Here’s what else you may need to cover:

  • Roof damage or leaks
  • Plumbing or electrical issues
  • Structural cracks
  • Mold, pests, or drainage problems

It’s smart to keep a cushion of savings to handle these without stress.

7. Be ready to act quickly—but don’t rush the decision

Great homes in desirable neighborhoods don’t last long—especially in a competitive market. While you shouldn’t feel pressured to buy something you're unsure about, it helps to be organized and ready when the right one comes along.

Keep your paperwork updated, stay in touch with your agent, and have a clear idea of what you're looking for. That way, when you find the one, you're ready to act—without hesitation or regret.

Final thoughts

Your first home purchase should be exciting, not overwhelming. With the right knowledge, support, and planning, you can enjoy the process and make smart, informed decisions. Use these seven tips as your guide—and remember, you're not alone in this journey.
